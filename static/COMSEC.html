Communications Security

Secure communication is an important aspect of business. Unfortunately, it's also constantly overlooked due to an " it won't happen to me" mentality. What is it? Regardless, by simply being aware and taking a few small steps, you and your business could be much safer.

Of course, the steps, however small, can only be taken by you. You decide if it's worth your time to keep reading, but consider not just the impact on your own life (e.g. your finances), but that of your colleagues and customers. Should your convenience really put them at risk?

I strongly recommend skipping ahead to "Introduction" and reading the entire page. The following short guide is meant for reference and quick start only—it features no personal choices or explanations.

Quick Start Reference

  • Use XMPP (and OTR encryption) for instant messaging.
    1. Download and install Jitsi.
    2. Use a company account or a personal account (sign up at one of the public servers).
  • Use PGP to secure e-mail.
    • Mac
      • Install GPG Suite.
      • Follow the simple official instructions or screencast.
      • Use Apple Mail with the included plugin, or go open source with Mozilla Thunderbird and the Enigmail add-on.
    • Windows
      • Install Gpg4win.
      • Consult one of many tutorials on YouTube.
      • Use Outlook with the included plugin—or go open source with Mozilla Thunderbird and the Enigmail add-on.
    • Linux
      • GPG comes pre-installed and you know how to work it.
    • Webmail (Gmail, etc.)
      1. Install GPG Suite (Mac) or Gpg4win (Windows).
      2. Use the Mailvelope extension for Firefox or Chrome.
      3. Get started.
    • Innovators & Early Adopters
      • Mailvelope is a free, open source, cross-platform, portable e-mail client that runs in your browser. User-friendly PGP encryption is under development.

Introduction

Is this ( really) necessary?

D, you used Skype! What's wrong now? Admit it's silly. Just use Skype, WhatsApp, Google, and whatnot. They're huge public companies, don't be such a paranoid douchebag!

The decision is yours, and most of us send clear text (readable by anyone) over the air every day without even realizing it. Statistically, we're either daredevils or morons.

Local crimes may often be physical break-ins, but keep in mind that technically—particularly when using WiFi near other businesses with visitors—an office easily becomes a naive duck.

With alarms and surveillance in place, yes, it definitely does make sense to also have at least a simple protocol for secure communication.

Threat Model & Requirements

The threat model is an overview of the threats surrounding a person, business, or situation. It is your compass as you evaluate and develop plans, policies, and solutions. For now, we'll simplify matters by documenting it as a set of requirements for safe communication.

  • Open source
    Free Open Source Software (FOSS) is subject to public scrutiny; its code can be reviewed, and its security verified on a source level. While this is a point of much debate, we (I) believe FOSS is highly beneficial.
  • Standards based
    Open standards benefit everyone, and the software is (or can be) available on more platforms.
  • Instant messaging
    The primary use case is “chat.”
  • Audio, video, and conferencing
    Necessary, preferably from the same application—separate software for IM and conferencing is a last resort.
  • Cross-platform and cross-device
    Different applications on different systems is fine if they’re compatible.
  • End-to-end encryption
    This must be possible for both calls and messages.
  • Message authentication
    Verification that the sender is who is says he is.

Protocol Evaluation

XMPP, OTR, and Jingle

The Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol (XMPP) is an open technology for real-time communication, which powers a wide range of applications including instant messaging, presence, multi-party chat, voice and video calls, collaboration, lightweight middleware, content syndication, and generalized routing of XML data. XMPP Standards Foundation

After evaluating what appears to be every standards based messaging solution available, XMPP is the obvious choice. It's arguably the only real standard for messaging, which explains its wide existing use. Jingle is an extension to give it multimedia capabilities.

It's field tested, diverse, and federated. As described by Wikipedia, it's also used for “publish-subscribe systems, signalling for VoIP, video, file transfer, gaming, Internet of Things applications such as the smart grid, and social networking services.”

Why XMPP

  • It can be extended with Off The Record messaging or key based security as necessary.
  • It can run via private servers—private domain and accounts.
  • It is federated (users registered on one server can talk to users on other servers).
  • It is what powers the Cisco WebEx platform, Facebook Chat, Google Talk and Google+, and much more.
  • It is the only widely adopted open protocol for instant messaging.
  • Is is being actively developed.
  • It has a massive community.
  • It has a massive amount of clients and servers for all platforms.
  • It has a massive amount of tried and tested extensions.
  • It has libraries for all major programming languages, including Ruby (web), Objective-C (iOS and Mac), Java (Android and desktop), and even JavaScript (web—with encryption, audio, and video).
  • It has Jingle, a widely used extension for audio and video, designed by Google (used in Google Talk) and the XMPP Standards Foundation.
  • It gives you flexibility to create new clients or complementary tools such as Jitsi's Videobridge for multi-user video conferencing.

Why Off The Record Messaging

Off The Record Messaging is a cryptographic protocol designed specifically for secure messaging. It provides the following features—as listed on its website—allowing private conversations (explanations are simplified).

  • Encryption
    AES encryption prevents others from reading your messages.
  • Authentication
    Using a shared secret, you verify the identity of the person you're talking to.
  • Deniability
    Messages are authenticated during a conversation, but after, anyone can forge a message. This makes it impossible to prove that a certain message came from a specific person.
  • Perfect forward secrecy
    Temporary per-message encryption keys ensure that if they're compromised, no previous messages are compromised along with them.

Software

Jitsi CK Recommended
Cross-platform with all features out of the box. Video conferencing over both SIP (VoIP) and XMPP. The only client fulfilling all requirements.
Trillian iOS
Available on many platforms, including mobile. However, not FOSS, OTR only on Windows (needs plugin), and audio and video only on Astra and Skype.
Vacuum IM CK
Russian site, but Google Code page in English. Only supports text, but is cross-platform, clean, plugin based, and has a good amount of features, including XMPP and OTR support.
Swift CK
Simple text-only client: cross-platform, built for corporate and military use. It has a fantastic set of goals on its website. Strangely, it seems to lack OTR support.
Adium CK Recommended
The go-to multi-service IM app on Mac, and most popular on this list. It supports a wide range of IM services, features OTR, and is very customizable with many themes and extras. It does not support audio or video, but is recommended for text-only messaging.
Pidgin Plugin CK Recommended
The most popular Windows client, and also available for Linux. It has OTR support through a plugin, but unfortunately, video and audio is limited to UNIX-like platforms (according to the FAQ). Recommended for text-only messaging if you install the OTR plugin.
Cryptocat CK Recommended
Since Cryptocat is a browser extension (for Firefox, Chrome, and Safari), it's available on all platforms except mobile. On Mac, it's also available on the App Store as a standalone application.

Great for setting up a quick chat with someone with no XMPP account, but its limitations are both positive and negative: it's all transparently handled for you, you can't specify conference room name or use your regular account. Still, how can we not recommend it?
Psi & Psi+ CK Recommended
Psi is a highly customizable open source and cross-platform client—one of the more advanced on this list. You'll want the plus version, since it supports OTR out of the box. It features not only OTR, but also key based PGP encryption. Recommended for those who want control.
Gibberbot CK Recommended
This application is part of The Guardian Project, creating easy-to-use privacy and security enhancing mobile software. It's a nice and simple text-only client: recommended.
ChatSecure iOS CK Recommended
One of the two applications available on iOS that feature OTR (IM+ is the other). Like Gibberbot for Android, it's a nice and simple text-only client: recommended.

Conclusion

Recommendations

Jitsi is the recommended solution available on all platforms (except mobile). It's simple to use, yet full of features, and will get you started quickly with secure messaging. Furthermore, it offers video, audio, and conferencing—even VoIP.

Alternatively, Mac users may install the popular Adium text-only client, and Windows users may opt for Pidgin. More advanced users may want to have a look at Psi+.

For launching quick chat sessions—especially for users with no XMPP accounts—the browser extension client (and native Mac client) Cryptocat is a great tool to have installed.

On iOS, ChatSecure is the client to download, while on Android, you'll want to get Gibberbot.

Beyond

On top of all this, if one wants a truly tailor made solution, there are both XMPP and OTR libraries (and open source inspiration) in most major programming languages—even JavaScript, for creating pure web-based solutions that run on any modern mobile and desktop platform.

As far as email goes, preferably you would encrypt every email with PGP, but at least those containing sensitive information. It may seem daunting at first but once you get set up, to actually use it is very simple.

A tip: consider using disposable email addresses (e.g. Mailinator—works with morg.cc addresses—and MailDrop) for signups to avoid spam.

Besides XMPP and email, there are a few other interesting solutions as well. BitMessage is a secure alternative to email, inspired by the technology behind Bitcoin. Then there are solutions leveraging anonymization networks like Tor, I2P, and Freenet such as TorChat, I2P-Bote, and Freemail.

However, for instant messaging, voice and video calling, and conferencing, XMPP is a time-proven solution that is suitably secured by Off The Record messaging.
